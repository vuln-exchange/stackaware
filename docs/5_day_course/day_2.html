For day 2 of the StackAware vulnerability management course, we're going to look at how to tackle the problem of vulnerability overload we explored in day 1

It all begins with a risk-based approach.

Vulnerability management is risk management

If patching software or otherwise fixing a vulnerability is easy to do, then just do it! No need to worry much about prioritization if it's as easy as updating your iPhone.

Unfortunately, for most businesses, it's not this simple.

Legacy applications, complex tech stacks, and concerns about downtime mean that you can't constantly be updating every system in your network. So you need to make tough decisions.

The key here is trading off risk vs. reward.

Vulnerabilities can create the risk of suffering a data breach or other cybersecurity incident. But your business needs to make revenue (the reward), so you usually can't knock a critical system offline to patch everything immediately.

Understanding which of the four risk management actions (mitigate, transfer, avoid, and accept) makes the most sense requires an understanding of the financial value to be gained (or preserved) in each case.

That's where quantitative analysis comes in; you need to put a dollar value on the risk posed by each vulnerability.

Attackers don't care how many "criticals" you remediated this month and neither should your business leadership. So take CVSS and just throw it out the window right now.

If you absolutely insist on using a non-quantitative approach, then check out DREAD or the Microsoft SDL Bug Bar. But I won't be going deep on these in this course.

The cyber risk management formula

There are many different ways you can get the financial value of a vulnerability and there are already some great existing frameworks such as the FAIR and the Hubbard/Seiersen approach.

While these techniques are relatively complex, you can really boil them down to:

Annual risk = annual frequency of cyber incident happening x financial impact of it happening

A more technical way of saying this is:

Annual loss expectancy (dollars per yer) = annual rate of occurrence (number of times per year) x single loss expectancy (dollars)

To abbreviate:

ALE ($/yr) = ARO (times/year) x SLE ($)

Note that ARO can (and generally should be!) less than 1, indicating that most incidents happen less frequently than once a year.

Additionally, it is possible to break down SLE further:

SLE = asset value ($) x exposure factor (percentage)

Because not every attack or event would necessarily result in the complete loss of an asset, we may need to scale the SLE by an appropriate value. That's the exposure factor. This will come into play in the next lesson when looking at some more advanced methods for calculating risk.

Applying this to the real world, and a caveat

"So what do I do with this formula?" you might reasonably ask.

I agree that the devil is in the details with these types of things, so stay tuned for the next lesson. In it, I'll explore three different methods for implementing it in real-world situations.

Before then, though, I need make an important point. Critics of quantitative cyber risk management will often say "there are too many unknowns to make a numerical estimate." And while there are certainly things we can't know, we do know some things for certain.

For example, CVE-2021-44228 (log4shell, probably the worst security vulnerability in history) is more likely to be exploited in the wild than CVE-2017-8283 (which cannot be exploited in the default configuration of Ubuntu, where it is often found).

If you have a problem with that statement, then I'm not sure what to tell you. Because you must also believe that there is no way to calculate the odds of getting abducted by aliens on the way to work vs. arriving safely after your morning commute.

My point here is that, yes, of course there is uncertainty. And no, we won't be able to make risk predictions with complete accuracy. You should hedge appropriately when communicating with others, making clear that what you are providing is an estimate. This is important when dealing with skeptics who will claim you are implying an unrealistic level of precision.

Both the FAIR method and Hubbard/Seiersen approach use more advanced methods of statistical modeling such as the Monte Carlo method to better represent this uncertainty. And you should by all means read their books if you have the time.

So know that, while I absolutely admit that the techniques I am recommending are not perfect, they are good enough for the vast majority of situations.

And certainly better than the status quo of CVSS.
