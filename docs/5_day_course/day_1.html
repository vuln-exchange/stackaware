Welcome to StackAware's vulnerability management course!

Over the next few days, we'll dive deep into one of the major security problems facing organizations today: dealing with known vulnerability overload.

In this lesson, we're going to take a look at the problem, and hopefully, show that you aren't alone in your struggles.

First, a definition

By known vulnerabilities, I mean those which a network defenders could reasonably be expected to know about through some sort of scanning tool or manual analysis. Check out this  post  for details, but know that this excludes "zero days" (at least prior to their public announcement) and other situations where an attacker is the only one who knows about the flaw prior to exploitation.

You'll need to use other techniques (like penetration testing) to find unknown vulnerabilities, and they are certainly important as well. But this course focuses on the known type.

There is a massive quantity of known vulnerabilities in most networks

Anecdotally, organizations can have  40,000 ,  130,000 , or more known vulnerabilities in their systems. Other practitioners have told me that they have seen networks with millions.

Dozens of new CVEs (just one type of known vulnerability) are  reported  every day in the National Vulnerability Database (NVD). And there is a huge chunk of known issues that don't even  get reported  there.

Exploitability of known vulnerabilities is difficult to gauge

Many, if not most of the entries in the NVD are " science projects " that were exploited under very narrow circumstances by researchers. As a result, multiple studies suggest that  90%  or more of CVEs aren't exploitable in a given configuration.

While most application security  tools  generally can't determine exploitability, they will generally flag libraries and components that have known vulnerabilities in them. This makes the signal-to-noise ratio very low and wastes valuable time on the part of informations security teams (e.g. you!) who need to triage a lot of issues that present very low risk.

The Common Vulnerability Scoring System (CVSS) is a broken industry standard

In addition to the fact that most vulnerability scanner findings aren't exploitable is the unfortunate reality that the way they are scored - generally with the CVSS - does  not  make sense.

Many organizations use a strategy of prioritizing "high" and "critical" findings (meaning they have a CVSS score of 7.0+) for remediation and leaving other issues for later (or never fixing them at all).

Despite  many   organizations , including the one that  created  the CVSS and the  United States Government , pointing out that this strategy is highly counterproductive, many security teams continue to pursue it or even demand their vendors do the same.

Enterprises are overwhelmed

Prior to being breached in 2017 via  CVE-2017-5638 , for which it was fined  $575 million , the credit monitoring company Equifax was using manual,  email-driven  processes to prioritize vulnerabilities for remediation. It also had a backlog of over 1000 known vulnerabilities on  internet-facing  systems alone. At the time of the breach, Equifax's market capitalization was over  $17 billion .

Let that sink in: a deca-billion dollar company safeguarding incredibly sensitive data was struggling to manage its vulnerabilities so much it was eventually breached by one known to be exploitable... two months  after being publicly announced!

But the thing is, this isn't rare at all.

According to one research study, most organizations appear to have a " natural limit " of being able to remediate only 10% of the total vulnerabilities in their network each month. So almost no organization, irrespective of size, will be able to just "fix everything."

How do I deal with this problem?

As you can see, basically everyone is dealing with vulnerability overload. And considering that attackers begin  exploiting  known vulnerabilities almost immediately upon publication, this means that prioritizing effectively is the single most important task in vulnerability management.

We'll look at how to do this beginning with Day 2's lesson.
